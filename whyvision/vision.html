Why Vision ?
Approximately 45% astronaut short-term and long-term missions to the ISS are reported to experience changes in near or distant visual acuity.
This phenomenon is called "Spaceflight-Associated Neuro-Ocular Syndrome" (SANS), where the microgravity environment causes changes to the eyes and brain, including increased pressure in the head that can affect the structure of the eyes.

1. Mission Performance
Astronauts perform critical tasks, like conducting experiments and repairs, requiring sharp vision for accuracy.
2. Navigation and Safety
Clear vision is vital for navigating the microgravity environment and ensuring safety, especially during spacewalks..
3. Long Duration missions
Maintaining good vision is crucial for missions like Mars expeditions, as changes due to microgravity can hinder task performance.
4. Dependeence on technology
Astronauts need good vision to use computers, control panels, and communicate with Mission Control effectively.
5. Health and wellbeing
Impaired vision can cause discomfort, headaches, and stress, affecting overall wellbeing during long missions.

A. Photoreceptor Outer Segments and Disruption of the RPE Monolayer in the Retina
Mice sent to space experienced significant retinal degeneration, including a reduction in the length of the photoreceptor outer segments and disruption of the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) layer. The mice exposed to the space environment showed shortened or missing photoreceptor outer segments, as well as disruptions in the RPE structure that was not properly connected or attached to the retina. These disruptions indicate that exposure to space conditions can interfere with the normal development of the retina in neonatal mice.
B. Disruption of Normal IPL Development
It shows that the inner plexiform layer (IPL), which connects bipolar cells to amacrine and ganglion cells in the retina, experienced a significant reduction in thickness in mice exposed to the spaceflight environment. In the ground control group, normal IPL development was observed from postnatal day 3 to day 15, with the average width increasing at each developmental stage. However, in the mice that flew in space, there was a decrease in IPL thickness at all developmental stages. This indicates shrinkage and degeneration of the retinal structure due to exposure to the space environment.
C. Loss of Ganglion Cells
There are 25 types of ganglion cells in the mammalian retina, varying in size and branching patterns. In the control mice's retina, the ganglion cells appeared round with abundant cytoplasm, while in mice that flew in space, the cells were elongated, formed irregular layers, had little cytoplasm, and appeared unhealthy. This indicates that spaceflight causes damage to the ganglion cell layer of the retina.
